## 1. Foundational Concepts & Definitions

### Computer Graphics

* 
**Definition:** The art, science, and technology of generating, manipulating, storing, and displaying digital images and spatial data via computers.



### Pixels vs. Texels

* 
**Pixel (Picture Element):** The smallest individual physical point or unit of programmable color on a digital display screen.


* 
**Texel (Texture Element):** The fundamental unit or smallest pixel of a texture map applied onto the surface of a 3D computer graphics object.



### Industry Applications (Sectors)

* 
**Entertainment & Media:** Video games, movies, animations, and special effects.


* 
**Computer-Aided Design (CAD):** Architecture, engineering drawings, and manufacturing blueprints.


* 
**Medicine:** Medical imaging such as MRI, CT scans, and 3D organ modeling.


* 
**Education & Training:** Flight simulators, virtual laboratories, and interactive learning environments.



### Types of Computer Graphics

* **Raster Graphics:** Images composed of a static grid of pixels (e.g., JPEG, PNG) where scaling down degrades quality.
* **Vector Graphics:** Images defined by mathematical formulas (points, lines, curves) that scale infinitely without losing clarity.
* 
**2D Graphics:** Two-dimensional, flat representations of digital images containing only width and height ($X$ and $Y$ axes).


* 
**3D Graphics:** Geometric representations of three-dimensional spaces that include depth ($X$, $Y$, and $Z$ axes) to calculate perspective, lighting, and shading.



### Role of Linear Algebra in Computer Graphics

* 
**Transformations:** Uses matrices to calculate translation, scaling, and rotation of geometric points.


* 
**Projections:** Converts 3D scene coordinates into 2D screen coordinate planes.


* 
**Vector Operations:** Employs dot products and cross products to compute vertex lighting, surface orientations, and camera positioning.

## 2. Hardware & Display Technologies

### LCD Screen Mechanism

* 
**Backlight:** A light source passes through liquid crystals.


* 
**Voltage Modification:** Electric currents twist or untwist liquid crystal molecules.


* 
**Light Polarization:** The twisted crystals block or allow light to pass through color filters (Red, Green, Blue) to render the target pixel image.



### Interlaced vs. Non-Interlaced Displays

* 
**Interlaced Displays:** Draws an image in two alternating passes—first rendering all odd lines, then all even lines.


* 
**Non-Interlaced (Progressive) Displays:** Draws every line sequentially from top to bottom in a single pass, drastically reducing screen flicker.



### Dedicated Graphics Processors (GPUs)

* 
**Advantages:** * Frees up the main CPU from processing image intensive tasks.


* Highly parallel architecture allows fast processing of millions of vertices and fragments concurrently.




* **Core Tasks:** Handled during rendering:
* 
**Vertex Processing:** Performing geometric transformations on vertices.


* 
**Rasterization:** Converting geometric primitives into structural pixel fragments.





### Framebuffer and Rasterization Relationship

* 
**Rasterization:** Converts 3D vector shapes and primitives into flat, 2D pixel-based fragments.


* 
**Framebuffer:** A dedicated section of video memory that holds the raw pixel color states generated by rasterization until they are pushed out to screen.



### Refresh Rate

* 
**Definition:** The number of times per second a screen updates or redraws its buffer image (measured in Hz).


* 
**Effect on Visualization:** Higher refresh rates make fast movements appear drastically smoother and reduce eye strain by eliminating noticeable flicker.

## 3. Imaging, Shading, & Lighting

### Synthetic vs. Physical Images

* 
**Physical Images:** Captures of real-world scenes using hardware devices like digital cameras or optical sensors.


* 
**Synthetic Images:** Computer-generated imagery completely constructed from mathematical models, code, or digital geometry.



### Shading Techniques (Gouraud vs. Phong)

* 
**Gouraud Shading:** Calculates lighting colors solely at the polygon's vertices and interpolates those colors linearly across the rest of the surface. This results in a blockier, rougher look under direct light.


* 
**Phong Shading:** Interpolates surface normals across the entire polygon face and recalculates lighting details for *every individual pixel*. This yields smooth reflections and realistic specular highlights.



### Reflections (Diffuse vs. Specular)

* 
**Diffuse Reflection:** Scattering of light uniformly in many different directions off rough, matte surfaces (e.g., chalk).


* 
**Specular Reflection:** Concentrated, mirror-like reflection of light in a single direct angle off shiny, polished surfaces (e.g., a mirror or chrome steel).



### Normal Vectors in Lighting Calculations

* 
**Role:** Normal vectors define the perpendicular direction a surface face points. Shading systems calculate the angle between this normal vector and the incoming light vector to determine surface brightness and reflection intensities.



### Ray Tracing vs. Graphics Pipeline

* 
**Graphics Pipeline:** A fast, forward-rendering technique that projects geometry sequentially onto a screen step-by-step; optimal for real-time graphics like games.


* 
**Ray Tracing:** A physically accurate rendering method that traces paths of light backwards from the virtual camera to its sources, calculating realistic reflections, refractions, and soft shadows.

## 4. Pipeline, Clipping, & Graphics APIs

### Clipping Concepts

* 
**Definition:** Identifying and removing geometric components that lie outside the viewer’s active viewport or clipping frustum boundaries so they do not render needlessly.



### Anti-Aliasing Causes

* 
**Cause:** "Jaggies" or stair-step artifacts happen when trying to represent continuous smooth vector lines or curves inside a discrete pixel grid layout. Anti-aliasing blends edge pixel colors together to soften this visual effect.



### OpenGL Architecture, Features, & Libraries

* 
**Core Features:** Platform-independent, hardware-accelerated, cross-language, and handles primitive rendering pipelines cleanly.


* **GLU vs. GLUT:**
* 
**GLU (OpenGL Utility Library):** High-level functions supporting complex operations like drawing spheres/cylinders or managing advanced camera views.


* 
**GLUT (OpenGL Utility Toolkit):** A window-system independent toolkit managing user inputs, window states, and system callbacks.




* 
**glMatrixMode():** Specifies which matrix stack (Modelview, Projection, or Texture) currently accepts upcoming spatial operations.


* **OpenGL Architecture Layout:**

```
+-----------------------------------+
|        Application Program        |
+-----------------------------------+
                  |
                  v
+-----------------------------------+
|      OpenGL API (GLU / GLUT)      |
+-----------------------------------+
                  |
                  v
+-----------------------------------+
|     State Machine / Pipeline      |
|  (Vertex Processor -> Fragment)   |
+-----------------------------------+
                  |
                  v
+-----------------------------------+
|     Frame Buffer / Display        |
+-----------------------------------+

```

### WebGL vs. Plain OpenGL Advantages

* 
**No Plugins Required:** Runs natively inside modern web browsers.


* 
**Cross-Platform Integration:** Works cleanly with standard web elements like HTML5 and JavaScript layouts.


* 
**Zero Local Setup:** End-users don't need to compile binaries or configure platform drivers manually.

### Projections

* 
**Orthographic Projections vs. Parallel Projections:** Orthographic projections are a distinct subset of parallel projections where the parallel projection lines intersect the view plane at exactly a 90-degree perpendicular angle.


* 
**Architect's Tool Projection Type:** **Orthographic (Multiview Parallel) projection** is optimal because lines stay parallel and dimensions remain true-to-scale for precise measurements.

### Line Equation Derivation & Plotting Algorithm

Given $y = mx + c$ with points $a(x_0, y_0)$ and $b(x_1, y_1)$:

#### 1. Deriving $m$ and $y$:

* **Slope ($m$):**

$$m = \frac{y_1 - y_0}{x_1 - x_0}$$


* **Intercept ($c$):** Using point $a$:

$$c = y_0 - m \cdot x_0$$


* **Equation for $y$:** Substitute $c$ back:

$$y = m(x - x_0) + y_0$$



#### 2. DDA Line Plotting Algorithm:

```text
1. Read the endpoints (x0, y0) and (x1, y1).
2. Calculate dx = x1 - x0 and dy = y1 - y0.
3. Determine steps = max(abs(dx), abs(dy)).
4. Calculate increments:
   x_inc = dx / steps
   y_inc = dy / steps
5. Set starting point: x = x0, y = y0.
6. Loop for i = 0 to steps:
     PlotPoint(round(x), round(y))
     x = x + x_inc
     y = y + y_inc

```

### Surface Normal from Vectors

Vectors $A$ and $B$ sit parallel to a sphere's surface at point $C$. The surface normal vector ($N$) runs perpendicular to both parallel vectors.

* **Formula:** Compute using the vector Cross Product:

$$N = A \times B$$


* **Component Formula:**

$$N = \begin{bmatrix} (A_y B_z - A_z B_y) \\ (A_z B_x - A_x B_z) \\ (A_x B_y - A_y B_x) \end{bmatrix}$$


* **Normalization:** Turn it into a unit vector:

$$\hat{N} = \frac{N}{\|N\|}$$

## 6. WebGL & HTML5 Programming

Draft Exam Implementation (Red Rectangle, 400x600 Canvas) 

```html
<!DOCTYPE html>
<html>
<head><title>WebGL Red Rectangle</title></head>
<body>
    <canvas id="glCanvas" width="400" height="600"></canvas>

    <script>
        const canvas = document.getElementById("glCanvas");
        // Contextualize WebGL context
        const gl = canvas.getContext("webgl");

        if (!gl) {
            alert("WebGL not supported!");
        }

        // Set clear color to red (RGBA: 1, 0, 0, 1)
        gl.clearColor(1.0, 0.0, 0.0, 1.0);
        // Clear the canvas to display the solid color fill
        gl.clear(gl.COLOR_BUFFER_BIT);
    </script>
</body>
</html>

```
