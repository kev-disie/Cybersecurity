## 1. Fundamental Terms & Classifications

### Distinguish between Secondary and Primary Data

* **Primary Data:** Fresh, original data collected for the first time by the researcher directly from the source (e.g., through your own surveys, interviews, or experiments).
* **Secondary Data:** Existing data that has already been collected, compiled, and published by someone else for another purpose (e.g., government census reports, journal articles, or company records).

### Target Population vs. Population Sample

* **Target Population:** The entire, complete group of individuals, objects, or items that the researcher is interested in and wants to generalize findings to (e.g., *all 2,000 employees at Homex Com*).
* **Population Sample:** A smaller, selected subset of the target population chosen to participate in the actual study to represent the whole group.

### Variables: Dependent vs. Independent

* **Independent Variable:** The cause or predictor. It is the factor you manipulate, change, or vary to see its impact on something else (e.g., *Training programs implemented*).
* **Dependent Variable:** The effect or outcome. It is the variable being measured or tested, which changes in response to the independent variable (e.g., *Employee performance*).

## 2. Research Problem Statements & Objectives

### Components of a Research Problem Statement

A good problem statement typically requires 3 core elements:

1. **The Ideal State:** What should be happening under perfect conditions (e.g., *"Staff retention should remain high to maintain productivity"*).
2. **The Reality (The Gap):** The actual problem currently occurring (e.g., *"20 out of 50 critical employees have abruptly quit Spectral International"*).
3. **The Consequence:** The negative impact or cost if this problem is left unsolved (e.g., *"This leads to lost operational efficiency and high recruitment costs"*).

### Sample Formulation (Based on Spectral International Case Study)

* **Research Title:** An Investigation into the Factors Influencing Critical Staff Turnover at Spectral International Limited.
* **Statement of the Problem:** Spectral International Limited relies heavily on its 50 critical technical staff to manage its oil and gas operations in Nairobi. However, the company is experiencing a rising trend of experienced personnel quitting, with 20 key employees leaving within the last several months for unverified reasons. If this high turnover rate persists, it will lead to severe operational disruptions, loss of institutional knowledge, and escalating recruitment costs, threatening the firm's market stability.
* **Core Variables:** * *Independent Variables:* Work environment, salary packages, organizational leadership.
* *Dependent Variable:* Staff turnover rate / employee retention.

## 3. Levels of Measurement (Scales)

| Level of Measurement | Explanation | Example from Papers |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Nominal** | Simplest scale. Used only for naming, labeling, or classifying categories. No natural order or numeric value. | **Place of residence**, **County of birth**, **Social Security Number** |
| **Ordinal** | Categories have a logical order or rank, but the precise distance between ranks cannot be measured. | **Military Rank** (e.g., Captain > Lieutenant) |
| **Interval** | Ordered numeric data with equal intervals between values, but **no true zero point** (zero doesn't mean "nothing"). | **Temperature measured on the Kelvin Scale** *(Note: Kelvin actually has an absolute zero, making it Ratio data. If the question meant Celsius/Fahrenheit, it would be Interval)*. |
| **Ratio** | Ordered numeric data with equal intervals **and** a true absolute zero point (allowing multiplication/division comparisons). | **Income**, **Age**, **Years of Experience** |

## 4. Sampling Techniques: Probability vs. Non-Probability

### Why Probability Sampling is Preferred

1. **Eliminates Bias:** Every member of the population has a known, non-zero chance of being selected, removing researcher favoritism.
2. **Representativeness:** It produces samples that accurately mirror the true population.
3. **Statistical Generalization:** Allows the researcher to confidently apply the findings from the sample to the entire population.

### Quantitative Sampling Techniques (Probability)

* **Simple Random Sampling:** Every individual has an equal chance of selection (e.g., pulling names out of a hat).
* **Systematic Sampling:** Selecting every $k$-th element from a listed population frame (e.g., choosing every 10th person on an employee roster).
* **Stratified Sampling:** Dividing the population into distinct subgroups (strata) based on a shared trait (like department or age), then randomly sampling from each stratum.
* **Cluster Sampling:** Dividing the population into separate geographic groups (clusters), randomly picking entire clusters, and studying everyone within those selected clusters.

### Qualitative Sampling Techniques (Non-Probability)

* **Purposive/Judgmental Sampling:** The researcher deliberately selects participants who possess specific knowledge or characteristics needed for the study.
* **Snowball Sampling:** Existing participants refer other individuals they know who fit the research criteria (excellent for hidden or hard-to-reach populations).
* **Quota Sampling:** Setting specific targets for different categories (e.g., needing exactly 10 managers and 10 interns) and finding people non-randomly until the quotas are met.

## 5. Data Collection Instruments & Methods

### Interviews: Advantages & Disadvantages

* **Advantages:** Yields deep, highly detailed insights; allows the researcher to clarify confusing questions; captures non-verbal cues (body language).
* **Disadvantages:** Time-consuming to conduct and analyze; expensive; highly prone to interviewer bias or social desirability bias.

### Qualitative vs. Quantitative Instruments

* **Qualitative Instruments:** *Interviews, Focus Group Discussions (FGDs), Open-ended Observations, Document Analysis.*
* **Quantitative Instruments:** *Close-ended Questionnaires, Checklists, Digital Metric Trackers, Experimental Logs.*

---

## 6. Research Validity & Reliability

### Validity vs. Reliability

* **Validity:** Accuracy. Does the instrument measure what it truly claims to measure?
* **Reliability:** Consistency. Does the instrument yield identical, steady results when repeated under unchanged conditions?

### Internal vs. External Validity

* **Internal Validity:** The degree of certainty that the observed changes in the dependent variable were caused *solely* by the independent variable, rather than outside confounding factors.
* **External Validity:** The extent to which the study's findings can be generalized out to real-world settings, other populations, or alternative timeframes.

### Question 6 (b): 5 Types of Biases in Remote Work Study

1. **Selection Bias:** Only surveying remote workers who are highly tech-savvy, leaving out those who struggle with technology.
2. **Response Bias:** Employees lying about being highly productive out of fear that their bosses might monitor their answers.
3. **Measurement Bias:** Using a flawed tracking tool that doesn't accurately capture real output.
4. **Attrition Bias:** If employees who are burnt out quit the company midway through the study, leaving behind only the highly happy ones.
5. **Researcher Confirmation Bias:** The researcher selectively looking only for data that proves remote work is good, ignoring evidence that shows it fails.
